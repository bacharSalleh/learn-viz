Training a neural network means finding the weights that minimize error. The network makes predictions, we measure how wrong those predictions are, and then we adjust the weights to be a little less wrong. Repeat millions of times, and the network learns.

A loss function measures how wrong the model is. High loss = bad predictions. Low loss = good predictions. The entire goal of training is to find parameters that make the loss as small as possible. Think of it as a score you want to minimize.

Step 1: You are Lost in Fog

Imagine you are standing on a hilly landscape in thick fog. You cannot see the valley floor, but you can feel the slope under your feet. The contour map on the left shows a simple quadratic loss surface. The bright dot is you - starting far from the minimum.

Step 2: Feel the Gradient

The gradient is the direction of steepest ascent. So the negative gradient points downhill:

-\nabla f(\mathbf{x})

This is the direction you should step to decrease loss the fastest. Watch the optimizer trace the steepest descent path on the contour plot.

Step 3: Take a Step

Take a step proportional to the slope. The size of the step is controlled by the learning rate \alpha:

\theta_{\text{new}} = \theta_{\text{old}} - \alpha \nabla L(\theta)

Small learning rate = cautious baby steps. Large learning rate = bold leaps. Notice how the ball moves with momentum now - using a slightly larger step size.

Step 4: Reach the Valley

Repeat the process - compute gradient, take a step, compute gradient, take a step - until you reach a minimum where the gradient is nearly zero. The optimizer has now converged to the bottom of the loss surface.

In practice, neural networks have millions of parameters, so this "landscape" exists in millions of dimensions. The intuition is the same: follow the slope downhill.

The gradient descent update rule is elegant in its simplicity. At every step, we nudge each parameter in the direction that reduces loss.

The Gradient Descent Update Rule
\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \alpha \nabla_\theta L(\theta_t)
\theta

Model parameters (weights)

\alpha

Learning rate (step size)

\nabla_\theta L

Gradient of the loss

But how do we compute that gradient? Through backpropagation - the chain rule applied systematically through the network. Step through the forward and backward pass below to see gradients flow.

Linear model: ŷ = w · x + b   (x=2, w=0.5, b=0.1)

Backpropagation is just the chain rule from calculus applied layer by layer. Each node multiplies the incoming gradient by its local gradient and passes it backward. This is what makes training deep networks tractable.

The learning rate is the single most important hyperparameter. Too small and training crawls. Too large and it explodes. Use the slider to see how loss evolves under different learning rates.

Vanilla gradient descent is just the beginning. Modern optimizers add momentum, adaptive learning rates, and other tricks to converge faster and more reliably. Watch all three tackle the challenging Rosenbrock function simultaneously.

SGD

Plain stochastic gradient descent. Follows the gradient directly. Can be slow in narrow valleys.

Momentum

Adds a velocity term that accumulates past gradients. Builds up speed in consistent directions.

Adam

Combines momentum with per-parameter adaptive rates. The default choice for most deep learning.

Adam is the safe default. It works well out of the box for most problems. SGD with momentum can sometimes find better solutions for image classification but requires more careful tuning. Try Adam first, then experiment.
